## Project Goal

PushAssist Pro is one of my proudest projects. I developed it during my final
year of study as an assistive shopping device for people who experience mobility
limitations due to age, pregnancy, arthritis, injury, fatigue, or other physical
conditions.

The goal was to make shopping easier, safer, and more independent. Instead of
requiring a user to push a fully loaded trolley with their own strength,
PushAssist Pro provides assisted motion so the trolley feels lighter and more
manageable.

## Why I Built It

Shopping is an everyday task, but it can become physically demanding for people
who struggle with walking, standing for long periods, or pushing heavy loads. A
shopping trolley can become difficult to control once it is full, especially in
large shopping centres where users may need to move through long aisles, queues,
and parking areas.

I wanted PushAssist Pro to support dignity and independence. The device was
designed around a simple idea: people should be able to shop comfortably without
needing to depend on someone else just to move a trolley.

## What PushAssist Pro Does

PushAssist Pro is designed to work with shopping trolleys of different sizes. It
helps the user move the trolley with less effort while keeping the interaction
familiar and easy to understand.

The concept supports users who may prefer to stand or sit while controlling the
trolley. That flexibility was important because mobility limitations are not all
the same. Some users may need light assistance while walking; others may need a
more seated or supported way to move through a shop.

## What I Built

- Assistive trolley-pushing mechanism
- Mechanical frame for attaching and supporting the assist system
- Motor and controller setup for powered movement
- User control approach for smooth start, stop, and speed response
- Power wiring and early safety checks
- Prototype testing focused on handling, stability, and user confidence

## Development Notes

The main design challenge was control feel. PushAssist Pro needed enough power
to reduce effort, but it also had to feel safe and predictable. An assistive
device should never surprise the user, especially in a public shopping
environment where there are people, shelves, corners, and narrow paths.

I focused on making the response smooth rather than aggressive. The device had
to feel like support, not like something taking control away from the user. That
balance between assistance and trust became the core of the project.

## Recognition

PushAssist Pro received strong recognition in 2024:

- Second place at the Botswana World Intellectual Property Day competition
- Second place at the Orange Summer Challenge

These awards meant a lot because they showed that the project was not only a
technical prototype, but an idea with real-world value. Both competitions
recognised innovation, practical impact, and the potential for technology to
improve lives.

## Impact

The most important part of PushAssist Pro is not the mechanism by itself. It is
what the mechanism makes possible: easier shopping, more independence, and a
more inclusive experience for people who face physical challenges.

The project reminded me that assistive technology should be practical,
respectful, and human. A good solution does not need to make the user feel
different. It should quietly reduce the difficulty of the task and give the user
more freedom to move through their day.

## Results

The prototype demonstrated assisted trolley movement and showed how powered
support could reduce the effort required during shopping. The recognition from
the Botswana World Intellectual Property Day competition and the Orange Summer
Challenge helped validate the project beyond the classroom.

PushAssist Pro became a major milestone for me because it combined engineering,
accessibility, empathy, and public presentation into one project.

## What I Learned

- How to design for users with different mobility needs
- Why assistive devices must feel predictable and safe
- How mechanical design and user experience affect each other
- How to present an innovation clearly to judges and the public
- How a final-year project can grow into a recognised real-world concept

## Next Iteration

- Improve the attachment system for different trolley designs
- Refine speed control and braking behaviour
- Add better battery and thermal monitoring
- Improve enclosure, cable routing, and durability
- Test with a wider range of users and shopping environments
