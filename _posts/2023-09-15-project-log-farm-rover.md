## Project Goal

Farm Rover was my first major robotics project. It began during the Technology
Transfer Makeathon, where my team built a rover platform focused on practical
farm use. The goal was to create a small mobile machine that could move through
a farm-like environment and support future agricultural tasks such as
inspection, monitoring, or carrying small tools.

I approached it as a learning project in robotics, mechanical assembly, motor
control, teamwork, and making hardware behave reliably outside a clean desk
setup.

## Project Summary

Farm Rover was built around a practical question: how could a compact rover be
used as a starting point for agricultural work? During the makeathon, our team
turned that question into a working concept by combining mechanical structure,
mobility, electronics, and remote control.

After the makeathon, the project did not stop. We continued working on the
prototype, refining the rover enough to present it at fairs and conferences
across Botswana. That extended life of the project made it more than a short
event build. It became a platform we could explain, demonstrate, and keep
improving from public feedback.

The project moved through three main stages:

- Makeathon concept development and team prototyping
- CAD planning, physical build, and drive testing
- Continued refinement after the event
- Demonstrations at fairs and conferences across Botswana

## Makeathon Origin

The project came out of the Technology Transfer Makeathon, an event where the
pressure of limited time pushed us to build quickly and make practical
decisions. The makeathon environment shaped the first version of the Farm Rover:
we had to divide work, make design choices as a team, test whatever we built,
and keep moving even when the prototype was not perfect yet.

That experience taught me how different a team hardware project feels compared
to building alone. Communication mattered as much as technical skill. Each
subsystem needed to fit with the rest of the rover, and every change affected
someone else's part of the build.

## What I Built

- Team-built steel rover chassis with a front protective frame
- Large-wheel platform for rough ground movement
- Rear carrying area for payloads or future farm tools
- Motor drive system for mobile control
- Basic control electronics and wiring
- Mobile control interface for remote driving
- Early testing workflow for movement, turning, and power delivery
- A platform that could be expanded with sensors or task-specific attachments

## Mobile Control Interface

One important part of the project was the control interface. I built a mobile
app concept that could connect to the rover and give simple drive control. The
interface included a speed setting, connection status, and a large on-screen
control area so the rover could be tested without needing a dedicated hardware
controller.

## Development Notes

Because this was my first project, a lot of the value came from learning how
small design decisions affect the whole machine. Wheel placement, wiring,
battery position, and motor response all changed how stable and useful the
rover felt during testing.

The project gave me a practical introduction to building systems where
mechanical design, electronics, control logic, and team coordination all have to
work together.

The CAD model helped me think through the platform layout before fabrication,
but the physical prototype taught the harder lessons. Once the rover was built,
the frame weight, wheel response, and control delay became much easier to judge.
That feedback shaped how I thought about later mobility projects.

## Prototype Testing

The prototype reached the stage where it could be driven and demonstrated. The
main test goals were simple but important: move forward, turn predictably, carry
weight on the platform, and respond well enough to remote control input.

## Public Showcases

After the makeathon, my team and I continued developing the Farm Rover and
showcasing it publicly. We presented the prototype at fairs and conferences
across Botswana, including the ILES conference.

Presenting it in person helped me explain the idea, get reactions, and see how
people understood the project when they encountered the physical machine rather
than only the design concept. Those showcases also made the project feel more
real: people could ask what problem it solved, how it moved, what it could carry,
and how it might be improved for actual farm tasks.

## Results

The rover became a working foundation for farm robotics experimentation. More
importantly, it gave me the confidence to keep building physical systems,
working in technical teams, and documenting the process from idea to prototype.

It also became the first project where I saw how a concept could grow from CAD,
to prototype, to public demonstration across real events.

## What I Learned

- How to think about a robot as a complete system
- Why power delivery and wiring layout matter early
- How much testing changes the original design idea
- How to turn a broad concept into a working prototype
- How to present a hardware prototype and explain its purpose clearly
- How mobile control changes the way a rover feels during testing
- How a makeathon prototype can keep growing after the event
- How public feedback helps shape the next version of a project

## Next Iteration

- Improve the chassis for uneven terrain
- Add sensors for field data collection
- Test longer run times with better battery management
- Improve the mobile app interface and connection feedback
- Refine the payload area for specific farm tasks
